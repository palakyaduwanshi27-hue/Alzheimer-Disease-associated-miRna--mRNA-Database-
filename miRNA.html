MicroRNA (miRNA) & Alzheimer's Disease

What is miRNA?

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small, non-coding RNA molecules that regulate gene expression by binding to messenger RNA (mRNA) and preventing protein production. They play a crucial role in various biological processes, including brain function and neurodegenerative diseases.

Role of miRNA in Alzheimer's Disease

Recent studies suggest that miRNAs are involved in Alzheimer's disease by regulating genes associated with neuronal health, inflammation, and protein aggregation. Key roles include:

1. Regulation of Amyloid-Beta (Aβ) Production

Some miRNAs control the expression of genes related to amyloid precursor protein (APP) processing, affecting the buildup of amyloid plaques, a hallmark of Alzheimer’s.

2. Neuroinflammation

miRNAs influence inflammatory responses in the brain. Dysregulated miRNAs can promote chronic neuroinflammation, worsening disease progression.

3. Synaptic Function & Memory

Certain miRNAs help maintain synaptic plasticity, which is critical for learning and memory. Their imbalance may contribute to cognitive decline.

4. Tau Protein Regulation

Abnormal miRNA activity can affect tau protein phosphorylation, leading to neurofibrillary tangles, another key feature of Alzheimer’s disease.
